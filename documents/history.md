# 10 Interesting Historical Facts

### 1. Cleopatra Lived Closer to the Moon Landing Than to the Pyramids
Cleopatra ruled Egypt around 30 BCE, while the Great Pyramid of Giza was built around 2560 BCE. The time between Cleopatra and the moon landing (1969) is about 2,000 years, while the gap between her and the pyramids is over 2,500 years.

### 2. The Shortest War in History Lasted 38 Minutes
The Anglo-Zanzibar War of 1896 lasted between 38 and 45 minutes, ending when the Zanzibari palace was destroyed by British naval bombardment.

### 3. Napoleon Was Once Attacked by Rabbits
After a peace treaty in 1807, Napoleon's chief of staff organized a rabbit hunt. They released thousands of rabbits, which, instead of fleeing, swarmed Napoleon and his men, forcing a retreat.

### 4. A "Phantom" Army Saved Rome
During the First Punic War, the Romans, with no navy, captured a Carthaginian ship and reverse-engineered it to build an entire fleet in just 60 days, saving their city from invasion.

### 5. The Olympic Games Featured an Art Competition
From 1912 to 1948, medals were awarded in architecture, literature, music, painting, and sculpture as part of the modern Olympics.

### 6. Vikings Used Sunstones to Navigate
Evidence suggests Vikings used "sunstones," crystals that polarize light, to locate the sun on cloudy days, allowing them to navigate the open ocean.

### 7. The Great Emu War Was Lost to Birds
In 1932, the Australian military waged war against emus that were destroying crops. The emus outmaneuvered the soldiers, and the "war" was lost.

### 8. The Fax Machine is Older Than the Telephone
The first working fax machine, the "Electric Printing Telegraph," was invented by Scottish clockmaker Alexander Bain in 1843, 33 years before Alexander Graham Bell's telephone.

### 9. There Was a "Year of Confusion"
In 46 BCE, Julius Caesar introduced the Julian calendar, creating a transition year of 445 days, known as the "Year of Confusion."

### 10. A Poet's Death Was Caused by a Turtle
The Greek tragic poet Aeschylus is said to have been killed around 455 BCE when an eagle dropped a tortoise on his bald head, mistaking it for a rock.
